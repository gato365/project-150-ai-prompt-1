1 / 6 — Why Does ANOVA Compare Two Types of Variability?

Imagine a researcher tests three different study methods on student exam scores. She finds group averages of 72, 85, and 91. But does that provide evidence the methods work differently — or could random chance explain those differences?

One-Way ANOVA answers: Are the differences between group means large enough — relative to the natural variation within groups — to count as real evidence?

To answer that, ANOVA splits all the variability in the data into two buckets:

  • Within-group variability — how much individuals differ inside the same group
  • Between-group variability — how much the group averages differ from the grand mean

The next four slides build each idea with visuals. Slide 5 brings them together in the F-ratio. Slide 6 is a summary before you start the questions.

2 / 6 — Within-Group Variability: The Noise
Within-group variability measures how spread out individual scores are around their own group mean (ȳᵢ). It is the noise — variation that exists even when all group members received the same treatment.

The strip plot below shows three groups (A, B, C). The y-axis is the response variable (Exam Score). Scores are highly variable within each group — the dashed lines mark group means (ȳᵢ) — notice they cluster near the center, so there is little signal but a lot of noise.

High within-group spread — each group spans nearly the full score range. Group means ȳᵢ (dashed lines) cluster near the center, indicating little between-group signal.

Key idea: Large within-group variability = individuals in the same group have very different scores from each other. This is the noise in ANOVA — it makes it harder to detect real group differences.

3 / 6 — Between-Group Variability: The Signal
Between-group variability measures how far apart the group means (ȳᵢ) are from the grand mean (ȳ̄) — the overall average across all observations. It is the signal — evidence that groups actually differ.

The same three groups now have tight within-group spread but separated means. The gray dashed horizontal line marks the grand mean ȳ̄. Arrows show each group mean's deviation from ȳ̄ — that is between-group variability.

Tight clusters + widely separated group means ȳᵢ = strong between-group signal. Arrows show deviation of each ȳᵢ from the grand mean ȳ̄ (dashed line).

Key idea: Large between-group variability = group means (ȳᵢ) are far from the grand mean (ȳ̄) — strong evidence to support a causal claim that groups genuinely differ. This is the signal ANOVA detects.

4 / 6 — The F-Ratio, Test Statistic & p-value
F = Between-Group Variability / Within-Group Variability
Test Statistic = MSBetween ÷ MSWithin  ·  Signal ÷ Noise

The strip plots below contrast a high-F scenario (clear group differences, tight clusters) with a low-F scenario (differences lost in noise). The y-axis is the response variable (Exam Score).

Separated means ȳᵢ + tight clusters → F >> 1 → small p-value

Overlapping groups, wide within-group spread → F ≈ 1 → large p-value

F valueWhat it meansp-value tendency
F >> 1Between-group signal dominates the within-group noise — strong evidence group means differ.Small (e.g., p < 0.05)
F ≈ 1Group differences are about the same size as random noise — little evidence the groups differ.Large (e.g., p > 0.05)
F < 0Impossible. Mean squares are always ≥ 0, so F ≥ 0 always.N/A
The p-value: Given a large-enough F, we ask — if all group means were truly equal (H₀), how likely is it to see an F this large by chance? The p-value answers this. A small p-value means the data provide evidence for a difference among means; it does not alone "prove" causation.

5 / 6 — The Equal Variance Condition
ANOVA assumes that within-group variability is roughly equal across all groups. This is called homoscedasticity or the equal variance condition.

The strip plots below contrast equal variance (ANOVA works well) vs. unequal variance (ANOVA is unreliable). The y-axis is the response variable (Exam Score); the width of each group's spread reflects its variance.

Similar within-group spread (ȳᵢ variances roughly equal) — equal variance condition met

Group B is far more variable — equal variance condition violated, F-test unreliable

Why it matters: ANOVA pools within-group variance from all groups into one estimate for the denominator of F. If one group is far more spread out, that pooled estimate is distorted and the F-test becomes unreliable. Rule of thumb: largest SD ≤ ~2× smallest SD.
