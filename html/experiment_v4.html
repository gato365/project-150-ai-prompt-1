1 / 6 — Why Does ANOVA Compare Two Types of Variability?

Imagine a researcher tests three different study methods on student exam scores. She finds group averages of 72, 85, and 91. But does that provide evidence that the methods work differently — or could random chance explain those differences?

One-Way ANOVA answers: Are the differences between group means large enough — relative to the natural variation within groups — to support a causal claim that the groups differ?

To answer that, ANOVA splits all the variability in the data into two buckets:

  • Within-group variability — how much individuals differ inside the same group
  • Between-group variability — how much the group averages differ from the grand mean

The next four slides build each idea with visuals. Slide 5 brings them together in the F-ratio. Slide 6 is a summary before you start the questions.

2 / 6 — Within-Group Variability: The Noise
Within-group variability measures how spread out individual response variable values are around their own group mean (ȳᵢ). It is the noise — variation that exists even when all members of a group received the same treatment.

The dot-plot below shows three groups (A, B, C). The y-axis represents the response variable (e.g., exam score). Each row is one group. Dots spread far across the range — that is large within-group variability. The dashed lines mark each group mean (ȳᵢ); notice they cluster near the same value, meaning there is little signal between groups.

High within-group spread — dots span the full range within each group. Group means ȳᵢ (dashed lines) are close, so little between-group signal exists.

3 / 6 — Between-Group Variability: The Signal
Between-group variability measures how far apart the group means are from the grand mean (overall average of all observations). It is the signal — evidence that groups actually differ.

Here the same three groups have tight within-group spread but separated means. The gray dashed vertical line is the grand mean. Arrows show each group mean's deviation from it — that is between-group variability.

Tight clusters + widely separated group means = strong between-group signal. Arrows show deviation from the grand mean.

Key idea: Large between-group variability = group means are far from the grand mean — strong evidence that groups genuinely differ. This is the signal ANOVA detects.
3 / 6
4 / 6 — The F-Ratio: Signal / Noise
F = Between / Within
Mean Square Between ÷ Mean Square Within

The F-statistic is a ratio of the two variabilities. The two dot-plots contrast a high-F scenario (clear group differences) with a low-F scenario (differences lost in noise).

Separated + tight clusters → F >> 1

Overlapping groups, wide spread → F ≈ 1

F valueWhat it means
F >> 1Between-group differences are large relative to within-group noise — strong evidence against equal means.
F ≈ 1Group differences are about the same size as random noise — little evidence the groups differ.
F < 0Impossible. Both components are mean squares (always ≥ 0), so F ≥ 0 always.
4 / 6
5 / 6 — The Equal Variance Condition
ANOVA assumes that within-group variability is roughly equal across all groups. This is called homoscedasticity or the equal variance condition.

The panels below contrast equal variance (ANOVA works well) vs. unequal variance (ANOVA is unreliable).

Similar spread in all groups — condition met

Group B far wider — condition violated

Why it matters: ANOVA pools within-group variance from all groups into one estimate for the denominator of F. If one group is far more spread out, that pooled estimate is distorted and the F-test becomes unreliable. Rule of thumb: largest SD ≤ ~2× smallest SD.
