The prompting techniques
What Groups B and C were doing — and why the difference matters
🤖 No Structure — Example Prompt
Can you explain within-group and between-group variability for ANOVA?
🧭 Structured RTF — Same Prompt
Act as a patient statistics professor. Teach me the difference between within-group and between-group variability in one-way ANOVA. Use a concrete real-world example and define all key terms clearly.

What is RTF?

Role: Establish who the AI should be ("Act as a patient statistics professor"). This shapes tone, depth, and vocabulary.

Task: Be specific about what you want ("Teach me the difference between..."). Vague tasks produce vague answers.

Format: Specify how you want the response ("Use a concrete example and define all key terms"). AI follows formatting instructions precisely.

The same underlying knowledge is available to both groups — but the way you ask shapes what you get back. RTF isn't magic; it's just intentional.

Within-group variability
The noise — how much individuals differ from their own group
Each dot is a student. Each bar is a group mean (x̄₁, x̄₂, x̄₃). The dashed line is the grand mean (x̄).
High Within-Group Variability

Lots of spread within each group. Hard to tell groups apart.

Low Within-Group Variability

Tight clusters. Group differences are easy to detect.

What within-group variability measures

Within-group variability captures how much individual observations scatter around their own group mean. It is the background noise of the test — variation that has nothing to do with which group a person belongs to.

In the F-ratio, within-group variability is the denominator. Large within-group noise makes it harder to detect real group differences.

Debrief · Slide 4 of 7
Between-group variability
The signal — how much group means differ from the grand mean
Between-group variability = deviations of each x̄ᵢ from x̄ (grand mean)
Low Between-Group Variability

Group means are all near the grand mean. Little signal.

High Between-Group Variability

Group means spread far from grand mean. Strong signal.

What between-group variability measures

Between-group variability captures how much the group means themselves vary around the grand mean. This is the signal — if the groups were truly the same, their means would cluster near the grand mean.

In the F-ratio, between-group variability is the numerator. A large numerator means the groups are genuinely different — or at least appear to be.

Interpreting F

Value What it means Implication
F >> 1 Between-group differences are large relative to within-group noise Evidence that at least one group mean is different — reject H₀
F ≈ 1 Between-group variability ≈ within-group variability No evidence of group differences — consistent with H₀
F < 1 Within-group noise exceeds between-group signal Rare in practice; suggests sampling variability or violated assumptions
F < 0 Impossible Both numerator and denominator are sums of squares — always ≥ 0. F cannot be negative.
"A large F doesn't prove the groups are different — it tells us the observed differences are unlikely to be explained by chance alone."
Debrief · Slide 6 of 7
The equal variance condition
Why ANOVA requires roughly equal within-group spread

The condition

ANOVA pools the within-group variability from all groups into a single estimate — the Mean Square Error (MSE). This pooling only makes sense if within-group spread is roughly equal across groups.

If one group has much larger within-group variability than others, the pooled estimate is distorted — and the F-ratio becomes unreliable.

✓ Equal Variance — ANOVA valid
✗ Unequal Variance — ANOVA questionable
Rule of thumb: If the largest group standard deviation is no more than twice the smallest, the equal variance assumption is reasonable. You can check this in R with leveneTest() from the car package.

AI, prompting, and learning
Three things that determine whether AI helps you understand statistics
Factor 1
The Technique

How you structure your prompt. RTF, chain-of-thought, asking for examples — technique shapes what the AI produces, independent of content.

Factor 2
The Information

What domain knowledge you bring to the conversation. The more precisely you can describe what you want to know, the better the AI's response.

Factor 3
Your Understanding

Whether you can recognize a good answer when you see one — and know when to push back. AI can be wrong. Critical evaluation is yours to do.

Using the right technique + providing the right information still doesn't guarantee understanding — that last step is entirely on you.
